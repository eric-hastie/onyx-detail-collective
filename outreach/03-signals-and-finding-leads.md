# Buying Signals & Finding More Leads Yourself

This list will run dry eventually. This page makes Rob self-sufficient: **what a good fleet prospect looks like, and how to find ten more before breakfast.**

## The buying signals — what makes a fleet a *good* target

Rank any prospect on these. The more boxes it ticks, the warmer the lead.

| Signal | Why it matters | How to spot it |
|---|---|---|
| **Branded / wrapped vehicles** | They invest in image → they'll pay to protect it | Logos, wraps, "our trucks" photos on their site |
| **Fleet of ~5–50 vehicles** | Big enough for recurring work, too small for national fleet-wash contractors | "Careers" page hiring multiple drivers/techs; "fleet of X" copy |
| **Can't easily shop-detail** | Mobile is the *only* practical option → less competition | Big vehicles, 24/7 ops, vehicles always deployed |
| **New or growing** | Expansion = new vehicles, no incumbent detailer | Recent vehicle purchases, new location, "now hiring drivers" |
| **No current detailer** | Open lane | Visibly dirty branded vehicles; ask on the first call |
| **High inventory turnover** | Constant recon demand | Used-car / BHPH lots, auctions, RV dealers |
| **Cleanliness = compliance or product** | Non-negotiable, recurring spend | NEMT, food trucks, exotic rental, black-car |
| **One dense location** | Many vehicles per stop = route-efficient | Yards, commissaries, auctions, dealer rows |

**The walk-up signal:** a dirty branded truck sitting in a parking lot *is* a lead. Note the company name and call them.

## Where to find more leads (repeatable channels)

1. **Google Maps, drawn to a 15-mi radius.** Search a category ("HVAC company," "used car dealer," "landscaping," "limo service") and work the pins. The map is your territory.
2. **Drive your corridors.** Stemmons (I-35E), Harry Hines, Forest Lane (Garland), R.L. Thornton, industrial pockets in Irving/Grand Prairie/Garland. Branded fleets park where they're based.
3. **"Now hiring drivers/techs" posts** (Indeed, ZipRecruiter, company sites) = a growing fleet. Hiring 5 drivers means 5 more vehicles to keep clean.
4. **Industry directories:** local HVAC/plumbing/pest-control association member lists, NEMT provider lists, dealer-license lookups.
5. **Instagram / Facebook local business pages** — service companies post truck photos; comment-worthy if the truck's dirty (politely DM later).
6. **Referrals from current clients** — the highest-converting channel once you have a few accounts. Every fleet owner knows other fleet owners. *Always* ask: "Who else runs trucks that I should talk to?"
7. **Commissaries & shared yards** — one stop, many independent operators (food trucks, owner-op truckers).

## A dead-simple lead tracker

You don't need a CRM yet. A spreadsheet (or the `leads/` files here) with these columns is enough:

| Business | Segment | Contact / role | Phone/email | Last touch | Outcome | Next step | Date |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:
- **Every** call/walk-up/email gets a row and a "next step."
- No lead dies without a hard "no" or 4+ touches.
- Review weekly: who's due for a follow-up?

This tracker is also the raw material for the **Phase-2 GTM plan** — it tells us which segments respond, what pricing lands, and where to double down.

## What to feed back for Phase 2 (GTM)

As Rob works the list, capture:
- Which **segment** says yes most (focus the GTM there).
- Which **opener / script** books the most trials.
- Real **prices** that closed (replaces the estimate ranges).
- **Objections** heard repeatedly (the GTM addresses them head-on).
- **Time per vehicle** in practice (sharpens pricing and routing).

Bring that back and the GTM plan writes itself from real data instead of guesses.
