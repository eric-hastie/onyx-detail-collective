Why fleet work — and how to win it

Retail detailing is a treadmill: every dollar needs a new customer. Fleet detailing is recurring revenue — land an account once, service it on a schedule, get paid predictably. Your edge as a mobile operator is simple: most fleets can't bring vehicles to a shop, so you come to their yard. That's the whole pitch.

The four kinds of fleet buyer

Image-driven

HVAC, plumbing, pest, exotic rental, black-car. The vehicle is a billboard. "A clean fleet sells for you; dirty trucks cost you jobs."

Volume / recon

Dealers, auctions, RV. They want units sale-ready fast. "I add recon capacity on your lot, per car, no bottleneck."

Compliance-driven

Medical transport (NEMT). Sanitation is regulated. "Compliance-grade sanitize + detail on a fixed schedule."

Facilities / ops

Hotels, clubs, property mgmt. One less vendor headache. "One scheduled vendor for every vehicle on the property."

The model that wins: trial → contract

STEP 1

Land a trial

Detail one or two vehicles at a fair price so they see the work, risk-free.

STEP 2

Walk the result

Show the owner the finished vehicle in person. This is where the contract is sold.

STEP 3

Propose a schedule

Per-vehicle price × cadence = one predictable monthly invoice.

STEP 4

Lock it + refer

One-page agreement. Over-deliver early, then ask who else runs trucks.

Outreach kit — what to say

Copy-paste starting points. The golden rule: lead with the problem you solve, ask for the decision-maker, propose a trial. Never open with price.

Cold-call opener — get to the right person

"Hi, this is Rob with Onyx Detail Collective. Who handles keeping your trucks/vans clean — is that you or someone else?"

"Great. I run a mobile detailing service — I come to your yard, so your vehicles never leave to get cleaned. I'd love to do one truck as a no-pressure trial so you can see the work. If you like it, we set up a schedule; if not, no harm. Could I swing by this week?"

Opener · Image-driven (service, exotic)

"Your trucks are out in driveways all day — they're rolling billboards. I keep fleets like yours photo-ready so every job site sees a clean, professional brand."

Opener · Volume/recon (dealers, auctions)

"I add reconditioning capacity right on your lot — per-car, on your schedule, so units get sale-ready without clogging your shop."

Opener · Compliance (NEMT)

"I run a recurring sanitize-and-detail program built for patient-transport vans — interiors disinfected on a fixed schedule, no downtime since I come to you."

Opener · Facilities/ops (hotels, clubs)

"I can be your single scheduled vendor for every vehicle on the property — shuttles, valet cars, maintenance trucks — kept to your brand standard, on-site."

Text — for owner-run small fleets

Hi [Name], this is Rob with Onyx Detail Collective — mobile fleet detailing in Dallas. I come to your yard so your trucks never miss a day of work. Can I do one as a free-look trial this week? Happy to send before/after pics.

Walk-up — dealer lots & yards (your best channel)

"I'm Rob with Onyx — mobile detailing. I detail recon units right here on the lot so you don't tie up staff or send cars out. Want me to do one right now so you can see it?"

Cold email — keep it under 120 words, attach 2–3 before/afters

Subject: Keeping [Company]'s fleet sharp — on-site, on a schedule

Hi [Name], I'm Rob with Onyx Detail Collective, a mobile detailing service in Dallas. I keep fleets clean without the vehicles ever leaving the yard — I come to you, on a set schedule. I'd like to detail one vehicle as a trial so you can see the work, no commitment. Open to a quick call this week? — Rob, [phone]

Follow-up is where the money is. Most fleet deals close on the 2nd–4th touch. Call/walk-up → email the one-pager (48 hrs) → "what day works?" (Fri) → a value text next week. No lead dies without a hard no or 4+ touches.

Pricing fleet contracts

Fleet pricing trades a lower per-vehicle price for volume + a recurring schedule. The math: per-vehicle price × cadence = a predictable monthly invoice. Ranges below are for structuring the offer — Rob sets final numbers from his own costs.

TierWhat's includedCadenceStarting range*
Maintenance WashExterior wash, wheels, windows, quick wipe-downWeekly / bi-weekly$15–$35
Standard Fleet DetailWash + full interior vacuum, wipe, glassBi-weekly / monthly$40–$90
Deep / Recon DetailFull interior + exterior, shampoo/extraction, dressingMonthly / per-unit$90–$200+
Sanitize Add-OnDisinfectant treatment, documented (NEMT)Per the contract$20–$50

*Starting ranges for structuring the offer only — not quotes. Lower end = high-volume single-yard; higher end = larger vehicles or deeper work.

Quote the schedule, not the car

"All 20 trucks kept sharp for $X/month, every other Tuesday" lands better than "$25/truck."

Volume + commitment discounts

More vehicles per stop → lower per-vehicle price. Small discount for a signed 3–6 month schedule.

Know your floor

(products) + (target hourly × time) + (drive ÷ fleet) = cost floor. Quote above it, every time.

Field notes — what working detailers actually do

The sections above are the fleet game plan. This is the owner-operator craft behind it — pricing, the rig, speed, and reputation — pulled from 20 recent interviews with working mobile detailers (2024–2026). It complements, not repeats: cold fleet outreach is in Outreach, contract pricing is in Pricing.

For the fleet game specifically

Hunt beyond the five segments

Operators grow into boats & marinas and private aircraft (helicopters/jets at private airports) — high-value, low-competition fleets a mobile detailer can service. Worth a search once the core list thins. — Hudson Archer

Let shops feed you

Performance/build shops and dealers funnel a steady stream of work — one operator's exotic jobs (McLaren, Lambo) all came through a single build-shop relationship. Partner with a few: they have the cars, you have the wash. — MJ Detail, Hudson Archer

Other detailers = referrals, not rivals

Established detailers route overflow and specialty jobs to people they trust. Introduce yourself to the local detailing community — one built a 6-year business almost entirely on word-of-mouth + detailer referrals. — Clean Car Chris / Archer

The multi-vehicle closer

A clean way to price a yard: 10% off the whole job when several vehicles are done same-day, same-location. It rewards exactly the density that makes fleet work profitable. — Top Star (pairs with Pricing)

Win image-buyers on-site

For fleets whose trucks are billboards, how you show up is the pitch: no blasting music at the yard, a folding table for mats (not the ground), and a branded A-frame sign (~$200) while you work — free advertising to everyone on the lot. — Hudson Archer

Fleet work forces you to scale

One recurring fleet contract can require hiring — a 2-year transport-fleet deal meant bringing on two people. Standardize for a crew: one pH-neutral soap, DI water in every rig, and pay base + an efficiency bonus, not flat hourly. — Archer, Hunter, Hudson

Price like a pro, not a rookie

Never race to the bottom

Survey the mid-to-top detailers in your area and come in ~25% under them while you lack reviews — but not rock bottom. "$40–50 details" are a trap you can't climb out of. — Top Star

Two tiers, "grandma-friendly"

An Express and an Executive (adds steam + shampoo). Don't build "a menu like the Cheesecake Factory" — too many options kills bookings. — Top Star

Less can close more

One operator dropped clay + shampoo from his core detail and raised the price ($250 → $305): "I get yeses every single day." Sell the deep stuff as add-ons. — Hudson Archer

Charge for time, hold a floor

Name your add-ons (engine bay +$65, clay +$75); bill anything 30 min+. Protect a ~$100/hr floor — a cheap $60 wash that eats 90 min loses money. — Hudson Archer

The rig — start cheap, scale on purpose

You can start on ~$600

Skip the tank and generator at first — run off the customer's water and power (a 100-ft hose + extension cord). Validate the business before you build a $10k rig. — Detailing Beyond Limits

Spot-free water isn't optional

In hard-water country, hose water "will leave spots." DFW water is hard — budget for a DI / spot-free setup and build it into the price. — Top Star (Vegas), applies to Dallas

60-gallon tank, not 100

If you add water on board, 60 gal is the sweet spot; 100 gal overloads a van and "will damage the transmission." — Hudson Archer

Battery beats a generator

An EcoFlow-type power station runs a full detail on ~30–40% charge — no noise, no fumes. A quiet rig is a selling point in nice / HOA neighborhoods. — multiple operators

Don't melt your plugs

A pressure-washer pump on a thin 50-ft cord will overheat the plug ends — use a 10-gauge cord. Hose/cord reels on your most-used lines save ~45–60 min a job. — IMJOSHV

The vehicle matters less than you think

Van, truck, or trailer all work — one operator details $140k Porsches out of a sedan. Don't over-build before there's revenue to justify it. — Clean Car Chris / Archer

Speed is the whole margin

Know your clock

Targets working detailers hit: Express < 2 hrs, full detail < 3 hrs, exterior + wheels ≤ 1.5 hrs regardless of vehicle size. Beginners take 5–6. — Top Star, Hudson, Hunter

80–90% first, perfect last

Don't perfect each spot as you go — you re-dirty it moving through the car. Hit it fast, then one final 5-min touch-up pass per zone. — Hudson Archer

Cluster your stops

One far-flung client can turn 3 cars into an 11-hour day. Group jobs geographically; recurring/well-kept cars stay clean, so each visit is fast. — Deep Scrub, Spotless Kings

Two-person split

One blows out + does the top half, the other vacuums + bottom half, separate towels — finish together. "If there's time to lean, there's time to clean." — Gueros

Don't over-book

Booked more than 2–3 days out means you're turning away same-day money. Treat a 3-days-out backlog as the signal to hire, not a flex. — Hudson Archer

Stage the night before

Refill water, top off bottles, stock clean towels, load drinks/ice before you leave — not at the first job. — Deep Scrub

Get found, get reviews, get referrals

This is the inbound / retail demand engine — separate from the cold fleet prospecting in Outreach. Both feed the schedule.

Google Business Profile, day one

Set it up before you buy a single tool — it's free and the #1 lead source. Fill it out completely; post a couple times a week ("Google rewards activity"). — Top Star

Reviews before ads

Get your first ~10 reviews from friends/family details. Running ads with a blank, review-less profile "is a waste of money" — prospects check, then bounce. — Top Star

A wrap is your best ad dollar

A wrapped van/trailer is a one-time "mobile billboard" vs. the "hamster wheel" of paid ads. Cheaper start: door magnets / decals from Vistaprint. — Top Star, Dro's (Dallas)

Be visible; carry cards

Detailing in a driveway or lot pulls walk-up quotes — it happens constantly on camera. Always have cards on you and hand them out mid-job. — multiple

Build a referral loop

20% off to the referred friend + $20 cash to the referrer. Fleet/retail owners know other owners — one happy client snowballs. — Hudson Archer

Answer the phone — or lose it

Pick up, or call back within 5–10 min; people "book with whoever answers first." On the call run Trust → Value → Urgency. — Top Star

Close on the walk-around

End every job with a walk-around; get a verbal "Yes, I'm 100% satisfied," then ask for the review (a QR card; follow up once, ask at most twice). — Top Star

Expectations > perfection

Bad reviews come from missed expectations, not bad work. Ask up front "anything you want me to focus on?" — that one thing decides the review. — Top Star

Don't get burned — technique & liability

Wash to avoid swirls

Two buckets + grit guard, wheels first, foam & dwell, then contact-wash top-to-bottom in straight lines — never circles (circles = swirls). — Hunter, Top Star

Beat the Texas sun

It's the chemicals drying on the paint, not the water, that spot. Work one section at a time, rinse sun-hit panels first, keep panels wet. — Hunter, Top Star

Liability landmines

Never dress pedals or rubber floor mats (slick foot → pedal → lawsuit). Don't soak screens/electronics. Don't touch customers' belongings — put it in your terms. — Hudson Archer

Acid wheel cleaner, carefully

Cool wheels in the shade, short dwell, never on forged/aluminum, and neutralize after with an alkaline rinse or it keeps eating the finish. — Clean Car Chris / Archer

Retire old towels

Replace drying/wash microfiber every 3–6 months — degraded fibers scratch paint. Color-code by purchase date and demote old ones to wheels/jambs. — Top Star

Finish where they look

Customers inspect wheels, glass/windshield, door panels, and trim gaps. Do interior glass after the exterior, and dress faded trim (most can't tell faded from dirty). — Hudson Archer

The boring stuff that keeps you in business

Get insured properly

Carry liability + "garage keeper" coverage, tell your auto insurer you work out of the vehicle, and confirm your equipment is covered. Note: wraps/logos can spike commercial premiums. — IMJOSHV

Finance smart, get an accountant early

Finance assets that cash-flow above their payment instead of draining your cash cushion — and hire an accountant sooner than feels necessary. — Hudson Archer

Lock up your gear

A stolen generator + compressor put one operator out of business for months. Secure the rig — your tools are the business. — Jay D's

Expect a slow ramp

"It takes 9 months to make a baby." Plan on 6–12 months to traction; don't compare your month 3 to someone's year 3. Get 1% better each car. — Top Star
