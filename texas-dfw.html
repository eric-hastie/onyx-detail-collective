Texas & DFW — what's different here

The parts of running a mobile detail business that change because you're in Texas and the Dallas–Fort Worth metro: the tax + licensing reality, the one regulation that can actually fine you, and how the local heat, water, and market shape the work.

Prepared for Rob · Onyx Detail Collective · Companion to the Fleet Playbook · Legal/regulatory items sourced from official TX agencies; climate/market items from operator research applied to DFW. Not legal or tax advice — confirm specifics before relying on them.

The legal & money basics

Good news: Texas is light-touch here. There's no special license to detail cars, and — contrary to what a lot of blogs say — you generally don't charge sales tax on detailing. The setup is mostly standard small-business plumbing.

No sales tax on the service

Car washing/detailing is not on Texas's list of taxable services (Comptroller pub 96-259) — so you don't add sales tax to a detail. Several "how to start" sites get this wrong. — TX Comptroller

…but products can be taxable

If you resell tangible goods (a bottle of coating, an air freshener), that sale can be taxable — and then you'd need a sales-tax permit (free to get). Confirm your exact mix with a CPA. — TX Comptroller

No detailing license needed

Texas has no state detailing/occupational license. Standard setup: form an LLC with the TX Secretary of State, get an EIN, and register a city/county business license (~$25–150/yr). — TX business guides

Register your local business license in your home city (Rob's base), not in every city you drive to — most jurisdictions don't require a separate license per job location. Verify with the specific city if a fleet account's yard is in a town with its own rules.

Wash-water — the rule that actually bites

This is the one to take seriously. As a mobile operator you're washing on other people's property and near storm drains — and letting that water go down the drain is illegal, with real fines. It's also directly relevant to your fleet targets, because you'll be doing this on commercial lots.

⚠ Soapy wash water cannot enter a storm drain — period.
Under the federal Clean Water Act and Dallas's stormwater ordinance (Dallas City Code §19-118.2, prohibited non-stormwater discharges), wash water carrying soap, chemicals, or road grime is a prohibited discharge into storm drains, gutters, and waterways. Storm drains run untreated straight to creeks — so this is enforced, not theoretical.

Capture or divert

TCEQ expects mobile detailers to contain and reclaim wash water (containment mats + a recovery vac) or divert it to a sanitary sewer/landscaping where allowed — not let it run off. Build this into your kit before you scale. — TCEQ

It's a fleet-account edge

Commercial sites care about their own stormwater compliance. "I reclaim my wash water" is a genuine selling point at a dealer lot or fleet yard — and the reason a low-baller with a hose can't service them properly.

Free help to get it right

TCEQ's Small Business Assistance line is 800-447-2827, and guidance doc RG-434 ("Environmental Rules for Car Washes") spells out the options. Worth one call before the first commercial job. — TCEQ

Built for DFW heat & water

Operators in hot, hard-water markets (Vegas, Phoenix, SoCal) kept hitting the same issues — and DFW shares them. These are their fixes, mapped to Dallas conditions.

Hard water → spot-free

DFW water is hard; on a hot panel it dries to mineral spots fast. A DI / spot-free water setup is non-negotiable for pro results — operators in Vegas say never use raw hose water. — Top Star, applied to DFW

Beat the summer flash-dry

In Texas heat it's the chemicals drying on the paint that spot, not the water. Work one section at a time, rinse sun-hit panels first, keep panels wet. — Hunter, Top Star

Shade + early starts

Operators run a pop-up awning and start at 7am to beat the worst heat. Daylight is a hard limit — a job that starts too late can't finish. — IMJOSHV, Out The Trunk

Near year-round season

Mild DFW winters mean you can work most of the year — a real edge over the seasonal northern markets operators warned about. Store compounds/polishes out of the heat. — Top Star

The DFW market

The metro itself is an advantage — if you don't compete on price.

Density is the tailwind

A Vegas operator named "3 million people within 30 miles" as his edge. DFW is more than double that (~8M) — plenty of fleets and recurring retail without driving far, if you cluster stops.

Affluent + HOA = quiet wins

Across Dallas's nicer neighborhoods and HOAs, a silent electric rig (no generator drone/fumes) is a selling point that lets you take early-morning and tight-residential jobs others can't. — Jay D's, Archer

Don't race the lowballers

DFW has plenty of $20-wash operators. Price mid-market and sell reliability + reclaimed water + insurance — the things a hose-and-bucket competitor can't match. — Top Star
